Where complexity becomes geometry.

Holography says a strongly-coupled boundary theory can conjure an entire bulk spacetime out of nothing but its own correlations. My work asks what quantum complexity — the resources it takes to prepare a state — has to do with that: whether the depth of the bulk is really tracking how hard the boundary theory is working, and whether gravity itself is a byproduct of that difficulty.

About

I work at the intersection of quantum information and quantum gravity — trying to understand what complexity, in the precise sense used in quantum computation, has to do with the emergence of gravity itself. If a boundary theory can encode a bulk spacetime, complexity may be part of what tells that spacetime how deep to be, and I'm interested in making that connection more concrete: through Krylov complexity, renormalization-group flows, and the geometry they trace out.

I don't limit myself to one corner of physics, though. I like following an idea wherever it leads, and I've worked on problems ranging from quantum random walks to astrophysical transients to the internal structure of large language models — partly because the tools of quantum information turn out to be useful almost everywhere, and partly because I just enjoy the range.
